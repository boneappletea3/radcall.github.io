What Is a Diagnostic Mammogram?

What Is a Diagnostic Mammogram?

A diagnostic mammogram is a specialized breast X-ray performed when there is a specific concern about your breast health. Unlike a screening mammogram (which is done when you have no symptoms), a diagnostic mammogram is used to investigate an abnormality that has already been identified.[1]

When Is a Diagnostic Mammogram Needed?

Your doctor may recommend a diagnostic mammogram if you have:[2]

• An abnormal finding on a screening mammogram that needs further evaluation

• A breast lump or mass you or your doctor can feel

• Breast pain that is persistent and focused in one area

• Nipple discharge or nipple changes (such as new inversion or retraction)

• Skin changes on your breast

• Changes in breast shape or size

• A previous breast biopsy showing certain findings that need monitoring

Age considerations: For women 30 years and older with breast symptoms, a diagnostic mammogram is typically recommended along with ultrasound. For women under 30, ultrasound is usually performed first, with mammography added if needed.[2][3]

How Is a Diagnostic Mammogram Different from a Screening Mammogram?

While both use X-rays to image the breast, diagnostic mammograms provide more detailed information:[1]

Screening mammogram:

• Performed when you have no symptoms

• Uses standard two views of each breast (top-to-bottom and side views)

• Designed to detect early breast cancer in healthy women

Diagnostic mammogram:

• Performed to investigate a specific problem

• Uses additional specialized views, including:

• Spot compression views (to separate overlapping tissue)

• Magnification views (to examine calcifications or masses more closely)

• Special angled views to better visualize specific areas

• Takes more time and images than a screening mammogram

• A radiologist is often available to review images while you wait

What to Expect During Your Diagnostic Mammogram

Before the exam:

• Avoid using deodorant, powder, lotion, or cream on your breasts or underarms on the day of your exam, as these can appear as white spots on the X-ray

• Wear a two-piece outfit so you only need to undress from the waist up

• Bring any previous mammogram images or reports if you had them done at a different facility

• Inform the technologist if you are pregnant or breastfeeding

During the exam:

• You will stand in front of the mammography machine

• A technologist will position your breast on a flat plate

• Another plate will compress your breast from above to spread the tissue evenly

• The compression may feel uncomfortable but only lasts a few seconds for each image

• Multiple images will be taken from different angles

• The entire exam typically takes 30-60 minutes, longer than a screening mammogram

After the exam:

• You may be asked to wait while the radiologist reviews your images

• Additional images may be needed if the radiologist wants to examine a specific area more closely

• This does not necessarily mean something is wrong—it simply means they want the best possible images

Understanding Your Results

Your diagnostic mammogram will be assigned a BI-RADS (Breast Imaging Reporting and Data System) category:[2]

Category 1 (Negative): No abnormalities found. Return to routine screening.

Category 2 (Benign): Non-cancerous findings identified (such as cysts or calcifications that are clearly benign). Return to routine screening.

Category 3 (Probably Benign): A finding with less than 2% chance of being cancer. Short-term follow-up imaging is recommended, typically at 6, 12, and 24 months.[2]

Category 4 (Suspicious): A finding that has a chance of being cancer. A biopsy is recommended to determine if cancer is present.

Category 5 (Highly Suggestive of Malignancy): A finding with a very high probability of being cancer. A biopsy is needed.

Category 6: This category is used when you already have a known breast cancer diagnosis.

What Happens Next?

Your next steps depend on your results:[2]

• If your results are normal (Category 1 or 2): You will return to your regular screening schedule.

• If you need follow-up imaging (Category 3): You will have repeat mammograms at shorter intervals to ensure any findings remain stable.

• If a biopsy is recommended (Category 4 or 5): Your doctor will discuss the type of biopsy needed. Most biopsies are done with a needle (core needle biopsy) rather than surgery and can be performed in the radiology department.

Important Points to Remember

• A diagnostic mammogram is more thorough than a screening mammogram and provides detailed information about specific breast concerns.

• Being called back for a diagnostic mammogram after a screening exam is common—about 10% of screening mammograms require additional imaging.[4]

• Most diagnostic mammograms do not find cancer. Many breast changes are benign (non-cancerous).

• Digital breast tomosynthesis (3D mammography) may be used during your diagnostic mammogram to provide even more detailed images.[2][5]

• If you are breastfeeding, you can still have a diagnostic mammogram. Try to pump or breastfeed just before the exam to improve image quality and comfort.[2]

Questions to Ask Your Doctor

• Why do I need a diagnostic mammogram?

• What specific area or finding is being evaluated?

• When will I receive my results?

• What are the next steps based on my results?

• Do I need any additional imaging tests, such as ultrasound or MRI?

Remember, your healthcare team is here to answer your questions and guide you through the diagnostic process. Don't hesitate to ask for clarification about anything you don't understand.
