What Is Breast MRI Screening?

Breast MRI (magnetic resonance imaging) is a specialized imaging test that uses powerful magnets and radio waves to create detailed pictures of your breast tissue. Unlike mammography, which uses X-rays, MRI does not use radiation. Breast MRI requires an injection of contrast dye (gadolinium) into a vein to highlight areas of concern.

Breast MRI is the most sensitive imaging test for detecting breast cancer, with sensitivity ranging from 71% to 100%—higher than mammography.[1][2] Because of this high sensitivity, MRI is recommended as a supplemental screening tool for women at increased risk of breast cancer.

Important: MRI Does NOT Replace Mammography

Breast MRI is not a replacement for mammography—it is an addition to it. Here's why you still need both:[3][4]

Mammography is the proven standard

• Mammography is the only breast imaging test proven in large clinical trials to reduce breast cancer deaths.[1]

• Mammography has been the standard of care for breast cancer screening throughout the world for decades.

• No studies have proven that MRI screening reduces breast cancer deaths—the evidence for MRI is based on its ability to detect more cancers, not on survival outcomes.[5]

Mammography detects cancers that MRI can miss

• In one major study, 13% of cancers were detected by mammography only and were missed by MRI.[6]

• Mammography is particularly good at detecting ductal carcinoma in situ (DCIS) that appears as calcifications—a type of early breast cancer that MRI may miss.[1][7]

• In a study of high-risk women, 65% of cancers found on mammography were DCIS, and 88% of mammography-detected cancers appeared as calcifications—findings that MRI often cannot see.[7]

• Certain subtypes of breast cancer, including some DCIS, invasive lobular carcinoma, and well-differentiated invasive cancers, may show little to no enhancement on MRI.[8]

The two tests work together

• Combining mammography and MRI provides the highest sensitivity (up to 84%)—better than either test alone.[1]

• MRI is better at detecting invasive cancers, especially in dense breasts.

• Mammography is better at detecting calcifications and certain types of DCIS.

• Using both tests together catches cancers that either test alone would miss.

Guidelines recommend both tests

• The NCCN recommends annual MRI in combination with annual screening mammography for high-risk women.[3][4]

• Many experts recommend alternating mammography and MRI every 6 months to maximize early detection.[3]

Who Should Have Breast MRI Screening?

Breast MRI screening is recommended in addition to mammography for women with certain risk factors. According to the NCCN and ACR guidelines, you may qualify for annual breast MRI screening if you have:[3][4]

Genetic/hereditary risk

• Known BRCA1 or BRCA2 gene mutation

• Untested first-degree relative of a BRCA carrier

• Li-Fraumeni syndrome (TP53 mutation)

• Cowden syndrome (PTEN mutation)

• Other hereditary breast cancer syndromes

Calculated high lifetime risk

• Lifetime risk of breast cancer ≥20% based on risk assessment models that include family history

History of chest radiation

• Radiation therapy to the chest between ages 10 and 30 (such as for Hodgkin lymphoma)

Personal history of breast cancer

• Diagnosed with breast cancer at age 50 or younger

• Personal history of breast cancer with dense breasts

Dense breast tissue

• Extremely dense breasts on mammography (especially for women age 50 and older)

High-risk breast biopsy findings

• Atypical ductal hyperplasia (ADH) or lobular neoplasia (LCIS/ALH) with ≥20% lifetime risk

When Does Breast MRI Screening Start?

The age to begin MRI screening depends on your specific risk factors:[1][3]

• BRCA mutation carriers: Annual MRI starting at age 25-29; mammography added at age 30

• Li-Fraumeni syndrome: Annual MRI starting at age 20; mammography added at age 30

• Lifetime risk ≥20%: Annual MRI starting no later than age 40, or 10 years before the youngest family member was diagnosed with breast cancer (not before age 25)

• History of chest radiation: Annual MRI starting 8 years after radiation, but not before age 25

• Extremely dense breasts: MRI starting at age 50 (can consider starting at age 40)

Note: For women ages 25-29 with genetic mutations, MRI alone may be used initially because of concerns about radiation exposure from mammography in very young women with certain genetic mutations. However, mammography is added at age 30 and continues alongside MRI.[1]

How Often Will I Need Breast MRI?

For most high-risk women, breast MRI is performed annually in addition to annual mammography.[3][4] Many experts recommend alternating the mammogram and breast MRI every 6 months (for example, mammogram in January, MRI in July) to maximize early detection, though this approach is based on expert opinion rather than definitive studies.[3]

Scheduling your appointment

• If you are premenopausal (still having periods): Your MRI should ideally be scheduled between days 7-15 of your menstrual cycle (counting day 1 as the first day of your period). This timing reduces background breast tissue enhancement and improves image quality.

• If you are postmenopausal or have irregular periods: The exam can be scheduled at any time.

- Inform your doctor if you have

• Any implanted medical devices (pacemaker, defibrillator, cochlear implant, insulin pump, etc.)

• Metal in your body (surgical clips, joint replacements, shrapnel)

• Kidney problems (the contrast dye is processed by your kidneys)

• History of allergic reactions to MRI contrast (gadolinium)

• Claustrophobia (fear of enclosed spaces)

• Pregnancy or possibility of pregnancy

• Medications: You can usually take your regular medications as normal. Ask your doctor if you have specific questions.

• Eating and drinking: You can usually eat and drink normally before the exam unless instructed otherwise.

• What to wear: Wear comfortable clothing without metal (zippers, underwire bras, snaps). You will change into a gown for the exam.

• Leave metal items at home: Jewelry, watches, hairpins, hearing aids, and removable dental work should be removed or left at home.

• Bring prior imaging: If you have had breast imaging at another facility, bring those images or have them sent ahead of time. Comparison with prior exams improves accuracy.

If you are breastfeeding

• It is safe to continue breastfeeding after a breast MRI

• There is minimal excretion of gadolinium into breast milk

• Consider pumping or breastfeeding just before the exam to improve comfort and image quality

Step-by-step what to expect

1. Check-in and screening: You will complete a safety questionnaire about metal in your body and medical history.

2. Changing: You will change into a hospital gown and remove all metal objects.

3. IV placement: A small IV (intravenous line) will be placed in your arm or hand for the contrast injection. You may feel a brief pinch and pressure.

4. Positioning: You will lie face-down on a padded table with your breasts positioned in openings in the table (breast coils). This position allows the best images and keeps your breasts still.

5. Entering the scanner: The table will slide into the MRI machine, which looks like a large tube. Your head will be near the opening, and you can usually see out.

The Exam

• The machine will make loud knocking, thumping, and buzzing sounds—this is normal

• You will be given earplugs or headphones to protect your hearing (some facilities allow you to listen to music)

• You must lie very still throughout the exam—movement can blur the images

• You can communicate with the technologist through an intercom at any time

• You will have a call button to squeeze if you need assistance

Contrast injection: Partway through the exam, the contrast dye (gadolinium) will be injected through your IV. You may feel:

• A cool sensation as the contrast enters your vein

• A brief metallic taste in your mouth

• Mild warmth or flushing (uncommon)

8. Completion: The table will slide out, your IV will be removed, and you can change back into your clothes.

How long does it all take?

• A full breast MRI takes approximately 30-45 minutes

• An abbreviated breast MRI (a shorter protocol used at some centers) takes approximately 10-15 minutes

Tips for Getting Through the Exam

• Practice relaxation: Take slow, deep breaths to stay calm and still

• Close your eyes if the enclosed space makes you anxious

• Focus on the music if headphones are provided

• Remember you can communicate with the technologist at any time through the intercom

• If you have claustrophobia: Talk to your doctor beforehand—medication to help you relax may be prescribed

Who Cannot Have a Breast MRI?

Breast MRI may not be safe or appropriate for everyone. You may not be able to have an MRI if you have:

• Certain pacemakers or defibrillators (though many newer devices are MRI-compatible)

• Cochlear implants or certain other implanted devices

• Metal fragments in your eyes or body

• Severe kidney disease (the contrast dye may not be safe)

• Severe claustrophobia that cannot be managed with medication

• Inability to lie still for the duration of the exam

If you cannot have an MRI, alternative supplemental screening options may include contrast-enhanced mammography (CEM) or molecular breast imaging (MBI).[3]

What Are the Benefits of Breast MRI Screening?

Breast MRI offers several advantages when used alongside mammography for high-risk women:[1][6]

• Highest sensitivity of any breast imaging test (71-100%)

• Not affected by breast density—works well even in women with dense breasts

• Detects cancers earlier—studies show MRI finds smaller tumors and more node-negative cancers

• Reduces interval cancers—cancers that appear between screening exams

• No radiation exposure

In a randomized trial of women with familial risk, MRI screening detected cancers at a significantly smaller size and with fewer lymph node metastases compared to mammography alone.[6]

What Are the Limitations of Breast MRI?

While MRI is highly sensitive, it has important limitations:[1][2][5][8]

• No proven mortality benefit—unlike mammography, MRI has not been proven in clinical trials to reduce breast cancer deaths

• Lower specificity than mammography (90-97% vs. 95-99%)—this means MRI may find areas that look suspicious but turn out to be benign (false positives)

• May miss certain cancers—particularly DCIS with calcifications and some well-differentiated cancers

• May lead to additional imaging and biopsies that turn out to be non-cancerous

• Requires IV contrast injection

• More expensive than mammography

• Not available everywhere—requires specialized equipment and experienced radiologists

If your MRI shows a finding that needs further evaluation, you may need

• Additional MRI images

• Targeted ultrasound to see if the finding is visible

• MRI-guided biopsy if the finding is only seen on MRI

Is the Contrast Dye Safe?

The contrast dye used for breast MRI is called gadolinium. It is generally very safe:[3]

• Allergic reactions are rare and usually mild

• The FDA has found no harmful effects from gadolinium retention in the brain to date

• The dye is eliminated from your body through your kidneys

• If you have kidney problems, your doctor will check your kidney function before the exam

Tell your doctor immediately if you experience hives, itching, difficulty breathing, or swelling after the contrast injection.

If your MRI shows a suspicious finding

1. Don't panic—most findings turn out to be benign

2. Additional imaging may be performed (ultrasound or additional MRI views)

3. Biopsy may be recommended if the finding remains suspicious

4. If the finding is only visible on MRI, an MRI-guided biopsy will be performed

5. Your doctor will discuss the results and next steps with you

The positive predictive value of MRI-recommended biopsies is approximately 15-40%, meaning that most biopsies prompted by MRI findings are benign.

Key Takeaways: MRI and Mammography Work Together

| | Mammography | MRI |

Remember

• Breast MRI does not replace mammography—it is used in addition to it

• Mammography is the only breast imaging test proven to reduce breast cancer deaths

• Mammography can detect certain cancers (especially DCIS with calcifications) that MRI may miss

• MRI is recommended for women at increased risk, not for average-risk screening

• Using both tests together provides the best chance of finding breast cancer early

• Scheduling during days 7-15 of your menstrual cycle (if premenopausal) improves image quality

• Most findings that require biopsy turn out to be benign

• Your healthcare team is available to answer any questions you may have
