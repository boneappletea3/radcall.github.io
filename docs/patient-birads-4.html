What Does BI-RADS 4 Mean?

Your recent breast imaging showed a finding that has been classified as BI-RADS 4, which means "suspicious." This category is used by radiologists to describe findings that do not have the classic appearance of cancer but are concerning enough that your doctor recommends a biopsy to determine whether the finding is benign (not cancer) or malignant (cancer).[1]

BI-RADS 4 findings have a wide range of cancer risk—anywhere from about 2% to 95%—which is why your radiologist may have further classified your finding into a subcategory (4A, 4B, or 4C) to give you and your doctor more specific information about the level of concern.[1]

What Are the BI-RADS 4 Subcategories?

Your finding may be classified into one of three subcategories based on how suspicious it appears:[1][2]

BI-RADS 4A (Low Suspicion)

• Cancer risk: 2-10%

• This means there is a low likelihood of cancer, but a biopsy is still recommended to be certain

• Studies show the actual cancer rate is around 7-8%[3]

BI-RADS 4B (Moderate Suspicion)

• Cancer risk: 10-50%

• This indicates an intermediate level of concern

• Studies show the actual cancer rate is around 22-28%[3]

BI-RADS 4C (High Suspicion)

• Cancer risk: 50-95%

• This indicates a high level of concern, though it is not quite as certain as BI-RADS 5

• Studies show the actual cancer rate is around 69-83%[3]

What Types of Findings Are Classified as BI-RADS 4?

BI-RADS 4 findings can include various abnormalities seen on mammography, ultrasound, or MRI, such as:

• A mass with irregular borders or other concerning features

• Certain patterns of calcifications (tiny calcium deposits)

• Areas of architectural distortion (abnormal tissue arrangement)

• Asymmetries that appear suspicious

• Other findings that don't look clearly benign

What Happens Next?

A biopsy is recommended for all BI-RADS 4 findings. This is the only way to know for certain whether the finding is benign or malignant.[1] The most common type of biopsy is a core needle biopsy, which is an outpatient procedure performed using image guidance (mammography, ultrasound, or MRI).[1]

During a core needle biopsy

• The area is numbed with local anesthesia

• A small needle is used to remove tiny samples of tissue

• The procedure typically takes 30-60 minutes

• You can usually return to normal activities within a day or two

• Results are typically available within 3-7 days

If the biopsy shows benign (non-cancerous) tissue

• You will typically return to routine screening mammograms

• In some cases, your doctor may recommend follow-up imaging at 6-12 months to ensure stability

• Certain benign findings (such as atypical hyperplasia) may require surgical removal or closer monitoring[1]

If the biopsy shows cancer

• Your doctor will discuss treatment options with you

• You will be referred to a breast cancer specialist

• Modern treatment options are highly effective, especially when cancer is detected early

Important Points to Remember

• A BI-RADS 4 finding does NOT mean you have cancer—it means a biopsy is needed to find out

• Most BI-RADS 4 findings turn out to be benign—overall, about 79% of BI-RADS 4 biopsies show benign results[3]

• The biopsy is important even if the cancer risk seems low—it is the only way to know for certain

• Early detection saves lives—if cancer is found, detecting it early greatly improves treatment outcomes

What Should I Do Now?

• Schedule your biopsy as soon as possible—do not delay this important test

• Bring a support person to your biopsy appointment if it would help you feel more comfortable

• Ask questions—your healthcare team is here to help you understand your results and next steps

• Try to stay calm—remember that most biopsies show benign results, and if cancer is found, early detection offers the best outcomes

It is normal to feel anxious while waiting for biopsy results. Consider

• Talking with family, friends, or a counselor

• Contacting a breast cancer support organization

• Writing down your questions before appointments

• Focusing on things you can control, such as maintaining healthy habits

Remember, your healthcare team is here to support you through this process and answer any questions you may have.
