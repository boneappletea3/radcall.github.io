Why We Don't Use Ultrasound Instead of Mammography

You may have heard about breast ultrasound and wondered why it isn't used as your main breast cancer screening test. This handout explains why mammography remains the standard for breast cancer screening and why ultrasound cannot replace it.

The Most Important Difference: Proven to Save Lives

Mammography is the only breast imaging test proven to reduce deaths from breast cancer.[1][2]

• Large clinical trials with decades of follow-up have shown that mammography screening reduces breast cancer mortality by approximately 23% in women ages 50-69.[1]

• No study has ever proven that ultrasound screening reduces breast cancer deaths.[2][3]

• While ultrasound can find some additional cancers, finding more cancers does not automatically mean lives are saved—what matters is whether those cancers would have caused harm.[2][3]

This is the fundamental reason why mammography remains the standard of care: it has been proven to save lives, while ultrasound has not.

What Ultrasound Cannot Do

Ultrasound cannot detect calcifications.[4]

• Many early breast cancers, especially ductal carcinoma in situ (DCIS), appear as tiny calcium deposits (calcifications) on mammography.

• Ultrasound cannot see these calcifications.

• This means ultrasound would miss many early, highly treatable breast cancers that mammography can find.

Ultrasound has a high false-positive rate.[2][3][5]

When ultrasound is added to mammography

• For every 1,000 women screened with mammography plus ultrasound, 37 more women receive a false-positive result compared to mammography alone.[3]

• 27 more women per 1,000 will undergo a biopsy that turns out to be benign (not cancer).[3]

• The biopsy rate more than doubles (increases 2- to 3-fold) when ultrasound is added.[5][6]

• In one large study, for every 1,000 women screened with mammography plus ultrasound, 52 women had a biopsy that showed no cancer, while only 5 women were diagnosed with breast cancer.[5]

This means that for every cancer found, many more women experience the anxiety, discomfort, and cost of unnecessary biopsies.

What the Research Shows

Adding ultrasound to mammography does not clearly reduce interval cancers.[2][7]

• Interval cancers are cancers that appear between screening exams.

• Studies comparing mammography alone to mammography plus ultrasound have shown no significant difference in interval cancer rates.[2][7]

• One large study found that adding ultrasound to mammography did not significantly increase cancer detection or decrease interval cancer rates, but did significantly increase false-positive biopsies.[5]

The benefits may not outweigh the harms.[5][7]

A major study published in JAMA Internal Medicine concluded: "These results suggest that the benefits of supplemental ultrasonography screening may not outweigh associated harms."[5]

Why Ultrasound Is Sometimes Used

Ultrasound does have an important role in breast imaging, but as a supplement to mammography, not a replacement:[4][8]

When ultrasound may be recommended

• To evaluate a lump or other symptom found on physical exam

• To further evaluate an abnormality seen on mammography

• As supplemental screening for women with dense breasts who cannot have MRI or other contrast-enhanced imaging

• To guide a needle biopsy

Important: Even when ultrasound is used for supplemental screening, it is always done in addition to mammography, never instead of it.[4][8]

What About Dense Breasts?

You may have been told you have dense breasts. Dense breast tissue can make mammography less sensitive, which is why supplemental screening is sometimes considered.[4][8]

However, even for women with dense breasts

• Mammography is still recommended as the primary screening test.[4][8]

• If supplemental screening is needed, MRI is preferred over ultrasound because MRI has a higher cancer detection rate.[4][8]

• According to the NCCN guidelines, "whole breast ultrasound may be done if contrast-enhanced imaging or functional imaging is not available/accessible"—meaning ultrasound is a last resort, not a first choice.[4]

• Ultrasound demonstrates a lower cancer detection rate than breast MRI or abbreviated breast MRI.[4]

The Bottom Line: Why Mammography Is Still the Standard

What the Guidelines Say

The NCCN (National Comprehensive Cancer Network) states:[4]

• "Although supplemental screening ultrasound detects more cancers compared to mammography with tomosynthesis alone, ultrasound demonstrates lower cancer detection rate than breast MRI or abbreviated breast MRI."

• For high-risk women who cannot undergo MRI, contrast-enhanced mammography (CEM) or molecular breast imaging (MBI) should be considered first. "Whole breast ultrasound may be done if CEM or MBI is not available/accessible."

The US Preventive Services Task Force notes:[2]

• "The use of screening ultrasound alone has not been evaluated as a method to reduce breast cancer mortality."

• Studies of supplemental ultrasound screening "reported additional false-positives and no differences in interval cancers."

Common Questions

"Ultrasound doesn't use radiation—isn't that safer?"

While it's true that ultrasound doesn't use radiation, the radiation dose from mammography is very low and has been proven safe. The small radiation exposure from mammography is far outweighed by its proven ability to detect cancer early and save lives. Ultrasound's lack of radiation does not make up for its inability to detect calcifications and its high false-positive rate.

"My friend had her cancer found on ultrasound—doesn't that mean it works?"

Ultrasound can find some cancers, especially in women with dense breasts. However, finding individual cancers is different from proving that a screening test saves lives across a population. Ultrasound also misses many cancers (especially DCIS) and leads to many unnecessary biopsies. The overall balance of benefits and harms has not been proven to favor ultrasound as a primary screening tool.

"Why can't I just get an ultrasound if I don't want a mammogram?"

Because ultrasound has not been proven to reduce breast cancer deaths, and it cannot detect many early cancers that appear as calcifications. Choosing ultrasound instead of mammography could mean missing a cancer that would have been found early and treated successfully. We want to give you the best chance of finding cancer early, and that means mammography.

Summary

• Mammography is the only breast imaging test proven to save lives from breast cancer.

• Ultrasound cannot replace mammography because it cannot detect calcifications, has a high false-positive rate, and has not been proven to reduce breast cancer deaths.

• Ultrasound may be used as a supplement to mammography in certain situations, but never as a replacement.

• If you have dense breasts and need supplemental screening, MRI is preferred over ultrasound when available.

• Your healthcare team recommends mammography because it gives you the best chance of finding breast cancer early, when it is most treatable.

Questions to Ask Your Doctor

Remember

• Mammography saves lives—this has been proven in large clinical trials.

• Ultrasound has not been proven to save lives and cannot detect many early breast cancers.

• We recommend mammography because we want to give you the best possible chance of finding breast cancer early.
