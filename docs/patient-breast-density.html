What Does It Mean to Have Dense Breasts?

What Does It Mean to Have Dense Breasts?

Your mammogram report may tell you that you have dense breasts. This is very common—about 43% of women have dense breast tissue.[1] Having dense breasts is normal and is not a disease or abnormality.

Breast tissue is made up of two main components

• Fatty tissue (appears dark on a mammogram)

• Fibroglandular tissue (glandular and connective tissue that appears white on a mammogram)

When you have more fibroglandular tissue than fatty tissue, your breasts are considered "dense." You cannot tell if you have dense breasts by how your breasts look or feel—breast density can only be determined by a mammogram.[2]

How Is Breast Density Measured?

Radiologists classify breast density into four categories based on how your mammogram looks:[3][4]

Category A: Almost entirely fatty (25% glandular tissue)

• Breasts are mostly fat

• Mammograms are easiest to read

Category B: Scattered fibroglandular densities (25-50% glandular tissue)

• Some areas of dense tissue scattered throughout

• Mammograms are relatively easy to read

Category C: Heterogeneously dense (50-75% glandular tissue)

• Much of the breast is dense tissue

• Small cancers may be harder to see

Category D: Extremely dense (&gt;75% glandular tissue)

• Breasts are almost entirely dense tissue

• Mammograms are most difficult to read

If you have Category C or D breasts, you are considered to have "dense breasts."[3]

Important: The assessment of breast density is somewhat subjective and based on the radiologist's visual interpretation.[5] Your density category may vary slightly between mammograms or radiologists.

Why Does Breast Density Matter?

Dense breasts matter for two reasons:[5][6][7]

1. Dense tissue can hide cancers on a mammogram

On a mammogram, both dense breast tissue and breast cancers appear white. This means a cancer can be hidden or "masked" by surrounding dense tissue—like trying to find a snowball in a snowstorm.[2][8]

• In women with extremely dense breasts, the sensitivity of mammography may be just over 50%—meaning mammography may miss nearly half of cancers.[8]

• Women with dense breasts have a higher chance of having a cancer detected between screening exams (called an "interval cancer") or at a later stage.[8]

2. Dense breasts are associated with a modestly increased risk of breast cancer

Women with dense breasts have a slightly higher risk of developing breast cancer compared to women with fatty breasts:[5][6][9]

• Compared to women with scattered fibroglandular density, women with heterogeneously dense breasts have about 1.3 times the risk of breast cancer.[6]

• Women with extremely dense breasts have about 1.8 times the risk.[6]

• Some studies suggest women with the densest breasts may have up to a 4- to 6-fold increased risk compared to those with the least dense breasts.[2][8]

However, it's important to understand that most women with dense breasts will never develop breast cancer. Dense breasts are just one of many factors that contribute to breast cancer risk.

What Causes Dense Breasts?

Several factors influence breast density:[2]

• Age: Younger women tend to have denser breasts; density often decreases after menopause

• Genetics: Breast density tends to run in families

• Hormones: Hormone replacement therapy can increase breast density

• Body weight: Women with lower body weight tend to have denser breasts

• Pregnancy and breastfeeding: May temporarily affect breast density

You cannot change your breast density through diet, exercise, or lifestyle changes.

Do I Still Need Mammograms If I Have Dense Breasts?

Yes—mammography remains essential even if you have dense breasts.[5][10]

This is critically important to understand

• Mammography is the only breast imaging test proven to reduce deaths from breast cancer.[5]

• Even in women with dense breasts, mammography still detects many cancers.

• No supplemental screening test has been proven to save lives—only mammography has this evidence.[5]

• Mammography can detect calcifications (tiny calcium deposits) that may indicate early breast cancer—other tests like ultrasound and MRI often cannot see these calcifications.[11]

The American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists states: "Currently, screening mammography remains the most useful tool for breast cancer detection and consistently has demonstrated a reduction in breast cancer mortality."[5]

Do not skip your mammogram because you have dense breasts. Mammography is still your most important screening test.

What About Supplemental Screening?

Because mammography is less sensitive in dense breasts, you may wonder about additional screening tests. Here's what you should know:

Supplemental screening is not automatically recommended for everyone with dense breasts.[5][10]

The decision about supplemental screening should be based on your overall breast cancer risk, not just your breast density. Most women with dense breasts do not need extra tests beyond regular mammograms.[10]

According to ACOG: "The American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists does not recommend routine use of alternative or adjunctive tests to screening mammography in women with dense breasts who are asymptomatic and have no additional risk factors."[5]

When supplemental screening may be considered

Supplemental screening is more likely to be recommended if you have dense breasts plus other risk factors, such as:[6]

• Strong family history of breast cancer

• Known genetic mutation (BRCA1, BRCA2, etc.)

• Prior chest radiation therapy

• Personal history of breast cancer

• High-risk biopsy findings (atypical hyperplasia, LCIS)

• Calculated lifetime risk of breast cancer ≥20%

For women with extremely dense breasts (Category D), the NCCN recommends considering breast MRI starting at age 50 (or age 40).[6]

What Supplemental Screening Options Are Available?

If you and your doctor decide supplemental screening is appropriate, options include:[6][11][12]

Breast MRI (Magnetic Resonance Imaging)

• Most effective supplemental screening test for detecting additional cancers[12]

• Recommended by NCCN for women with extremely dense breasts[6]

• Requires IV contrast injection

• More expensive and not available everywhere

• Higher rate of false-positive findings

Abbreviated Breast MRI

• Shorter version of full MRI with similar cancer detection[6][8]

• May be more accessible and less expensive than full MRI

Contrast-Enhanced Mammography (CEM)

• Mammogram performed with IV contrast

• May have similar performance to MRI[8]

• Alternative for women who cannot have MRI

Molecular Breast Imaging (MBI)

• Uses a radioactive tracer to detect cancers

• Higher radiation dose than mammography[11]

• Alternative for women who cannot have MRI

Whole Breast Ultrasound

• No radiation or contrast required

• Less effective than MRI for detecting cancers[6][12]

• Higher rate of false-positive findings

• Should only be used if MRI, CEM, or MBI are not available[6]

Important: According to NCCN guidelines, "Although supplemental screening ultrasound detects more cancers compared to mammography with tomosynthesis alone, ultrasound demonstrates lower cancer detection rate than breast MRI or abbreviated breast MRI." Ultrasound is considered a last resort when other options are not available.[6]

What Are the Downsides of Supplemental Screening?

Supplemental screening tests can find more cancers, but they also have significant downsides:[10][13]

• More false alarms: Additional tests often find things that look suspicious but turn out to be benign

• More biopsies: You may need biopsies that show no cancer

• Overdiagnosis: Some cancers found may never have caused problems (finding cancers that didn't need to be found)

• Anxiety and stress: More testing can cause more worry

• Cost: Supplemental tests may not be covered by insurance

• Time: Additional appointments and procedures

A modeling study found that adding MRI to mammography for all women with dense breasts nearly doubled the rate of false-positive biopsy recommendations.[13] The best balance of benefits and harms may be achieved by targeting MRI to women with extremely dense breasts rather than all women with dense breasts.[13]

What Does the New Federal Law Require?

As of September 2024, the FDA requires all mammography facilities to notify you about your breast density.[1][9] Your mammogram report will tell you whether your breasts are "dense" or "not dense" and explain that dense tissue can make it harder to find cancer on a mammogram.

This notification is meant to encourage you to talk with your doctor about your breast density and whether any additional screening might be right for you based on your overall risk factors.

Important: The notification requirement does not mean that everyone with dense breasts needs supplemental screening. It simply ensures you have information to discuss with your healthcare provider.

What Should I Do If I Have Dense Breasts?

1. Continue getting your regular mammograms

• Mammography is still your most important screening test

• Do not skip mammograms because you have dense breasts

2. Talk to your doctor about your overall breast cancer risk

• Breast density is just one factor

• Your doctor can assess your complete risk profile

• Risk assessment tools can estimate your lifetime risk of breast cancer

3. Discuss whether supplemental screening is right for you

• Consider your overall risk, not just breast density

• Understand the benefits AND the downsides of additional testing

• Make a shared decision with your healthcare provider

4. Know your family history

• Tell your doctor about any relatives with breast or ovarian cancer

• Family history affects your overall risk and screening recommendations

5. Report any breast changes promptly

• Be familiar with how your breasts normally look and feel

• Report any new lumps, skin changes, nipple discharge, or other changes to your doctor right away

Questions to Ask Your Doctor

Key Takeaways

• Dense breasts are common and normal—about 43% of women have them

• Dense breasts can make mammograms harder to read AND are associated with a modestly increased breast cancer risk

• Mammography is still essential—it is the only screening test proven to reduce breast cancer deaths

• Not everyone with dense breasts needs supplemental screening—the decision depends on your overall risk

• If supplemental screening is needed, MRI is more effective than ultrasound

• Talk to your doctor about your complete risk profile, not just your breast density

• The goal is to make an informed decision together with your healthcare provider

Remember

Having dense breasts does not mean you will get breast cancer. Most women with dense breasts never develop breast cancer. Continue your regular mammograms, discuss your overall risk with your doctor, and together you can decide if any additional screening is right for you.

Your healthcare team is here to answer your questions and help you make the best decisions for your health.
