Why This Information Matters

You may have heard about alternative methods for detecting breast cancer that are marketed as safer, more comfortable, or more effective than mammography. It is important to understand that mammography is the only breast cancer screening method proven to reduce deaths from breast cancer.[1] While some alternative technologies may sound appealing, they have not been shown to be effective for breast cancer screening and are not recommended by major medical organizations.

This handout explains why certain methods are not approved or recommended for breast cancer screening, so you can make informed decisions about your health.

For a screening test to be recommended, it must meet several important criteria

• Sensitivity: The ability to correctly identify people who have cancer (avoiding "false negatives" that miss cancers)

• Specificity: The ability to correctly identify people who do not have cancer (avoiding "false positives" that lead to unnecessary worry and procedures)

• Proven mortality benefit: Evidence from large, well-designed studies showing that the test actually saves lives by detecting cancer early enough to improve outcomes

Mammography has been extensively studied and has been shown to reduce breast cancer deaths.[1] The alternative methods described below have not met these standards.

Thermography (Infrared Thermal Imaging)

What it is: Thermography uses infrared cameras to detect heat patterns on the surface of the breast. The theory is that cancerous tissue generates more heat due to increased blood flow and metabolic activity.

Why it is not recommended

The National Comprehensive Cancer Network (NCCN) states that "current evidence does not support the use of thermography as a screening procedure." A systematic review found that thermography has highly variable sensitivity (25% to 97%) and specificity (12% to 85%), meaning it both misses many cancers and produces many false-positive results.[1][2]

A classic study published in the New England Journal of Medicine found that thermography had a true-positive rate of only 24% for detecting early-stage breast cancers, meaning it missed about three out of every four cancers.[3] More recent studies have confirmed that thermography has low sensitivity (as low as 25%) for detecting breast cancer.[4]

The FDA has warned consumers that thermography is not a replacement for mammography and should not be used as a standalone screening tool.[5] Despite marketing claims, thermography has never been shown to be effective as a screening method for breast cancer.

Bottom line: Thermography may miss breast cancers, especially early-stage cancers when treatment is most effective. Using thermography instead of mammography could delay your diagnosis and worsen your outcome.

QT Ultrasound (Quantitative Transmission Ultrasound Tomography)

What it is: QT ultrasound, also called quantitative transmission (QT) imaging or ultrasound tomography, is an emerging technology that uses sound waves to create three-dimensional images of the breast. Unlike standard ultrasound, QT imaging uses a ring of ultrasound transducers to capture images from multiple angles, providing quantitative information about tissue properties such as sound speed and density.[6][7]

Current status

QT ultrasound is an investigational technology that is still being studied. Early research suggests it may have potential as a breast imaging tool, particularly for younger women who are not yet candidates for mammography.[8] In one study comparing QT ultrasound to digital breast tomosynthesis (DBT), QT imaging showed non-inferior performance for detecting breast lesions and had a lower non-cancer recall rate.[8][9]

Why it is not yet recommended for routine screening

• QT ultrasound has not been proven to reduce breast cancer deaths

• Studies to date have been small and limited[9]

• It is not included in any major screening guidelines from organizations such as the NCCN, ACR, or USPSTF

• It is not widely available and remains primarily a research tool[6]

• Long-term outcomes data are lacking

Bottom line: While QT ultrasound is a promising emerging technology, it has not been validated for routine breast cancer screening. It should not be used as a replacement for mammography until larger studies demonstrate its effectiveness and it receives guideline endorsement.

Automated Whole Breast Ultrasound (ABUS)

Automated Whole Breast Ultrasound (ABUS)

What it is: Automated breast ultrasound (ABUS) uses a mechanically driven ultrasound transducer to capture standardized, three-dimensional images of the entire breast. Unlike handheld ultrasound, ABUS is less operator-dependent and produces reproducible images that can be compared over time.[10][11]

Current status

ABUS is FDA-approved as a supplemental screening tool for women with dense breasts who have had a negative mammogram. It is not approved or recommended as a standalone screening method to replace mammography.[10]

What the evidence shows

• ABUS can detect additional mammographically occult cancers in women with dense breasts, with an incremental cancer detection rate of approximately 2-4 per 1,000 women.[12][13]

• Most cancers detected by ABUS are small, invasive, and node-negative.[12]

• However, the BRAID trial found that ABUS detected significantly fewer cancers than abbreviated MRI or contrast-enhanced mammography (4.2 per 1,000 vs. 17.4 per 1,000 for abbreviated MRI).[12]

• ABUS increases false-positive findings and recall rates compared to mammography alone.[10][11]

Why it is not recommended as a standalone screening method

• ABUS has not been proven to reduce breast cancer deaths

• It has a lower cancer detection rate than MRI or contrast-enhanced mammography[12]

• It cannot detect calcifications, which are an important sign of some breast cancers[11]

• It is intended to be used in addition to mammography, not as a replacement

Bottom line: ABUS may be offered as a supplemental screening tool for women with dense breasts after a negative mammogram, but it should not replace mammography. If you have dense breasts and are considering supplemental screening, discuss your options with your doctor.

Molecular Breast Imaging (MBI) and Breast-Specific Gamma Imaging (BSGI)

What they are: Molecular breast imaging (MBI) and breast-specific gamma imaging (BSGI) are nuclear medicine techniques that use an injected radioactive tracer (technetium-99m sestamibi) to detect metabolically active areas in the breast that may indicate cancer.[14][15]

Current status

MBI and BSGI are not recommended for routine breast cancer screening in average-risk women.[16] However, MBI may be considered as a supplemental screening option for high-risk women who cannot undergo breast MRI.[1]

What the evidence shows

• MBI can detect additional cancers in women with dense breasts, with an incremental cancer detection rate of approximately 3.5-6.7 per 1,000 women when added to mammography.[17][18]

• The Density MATTERS trial found that adding MBI to digital breast tomosynthesis increased invasive cancer detection by 2.5-fold.[17]

• MBI has similar sensitivity but improved specificity compared to ultrasound.[1]

Why it is not recommended for routine screening

• MBI involves radiation exposure that is higher than standard mammography (approximately 4-fold higher whole-body effective dose).[1][19]

• There is insufficient evidence to support its use in average-risk women.[16]

• It requires an intravenous injection and takes approximately 40 minutes.[16]

• It has not been proven to reduce breast cancer deaths

Bottom line: MBI may be an option for high-risk women who cannot undergo breast MRI, but it is not recommended for routine screening due to radiation exposure and limited evidence. Discuss with your doctor whether MBI is appropriate for your situation.

Contras-Enhanced Mammography (CEM)

What it is: Contrast-enhanced mammography (CEM) combines standard mammography with an intravenous injection of iodinated contrast material to highlight areas of increased blood flow that may indicate cancer.[20][21]

Current status

CEM is an emerging supplemental screening tool that shows promise for women at increased risk of breast cancer. It is not yet recommended for routine screening in average-risk women due to limited data.[1]

What the evidence shows

• CEM has demonstrated superior sensitivity compared to standard mammography (87.5%-90.5% vs. 50.0%-52.4%) and achieved an incremental cancer detection rate of 6.6 to 19.2 per 1,000 in women at elevated risk.[13][22]

• The BRAID trial found that CEM detected cancers at rates similar to abbreviated MRI (19.2 per 1,000 vs. 17.4 per 1,000).[12]

• CEM may be noninferior to breast MRI for cancer detection.[23]

Why it is not yet recommended for routine screening

• Limited data exist regarding its use for breast cancer screening in average-risk women.[1]

• It carries a risk of contrast reactions (approximately 0.82% of patients experience a reaction).[13]

• It involves a slightly increased radiation dose compared to standard mammography.[21]

• It has not been proven to reduce breast cancer deaths

Current guideline recommendations: NCCN guidelines state that CEM may be considered for high-risk women who cannot undergo MRI.[1] The ACR-sponsored CMIST trial is currently evaluating CEM for screening women with dense breasts.[13]

Bottom line: CEM is a promising technology that may become a screening option for women at increased risk, but more research is needed. It is not currently recommended for routine screening in average-risk women.

Breast Self-Examination (BSE)

The U.S. Preventive Services Task Force (USPSTF), American Academy of Family Physicians (AAFP), and American Cancer Society (ACS) recommend against teaching breast self-examination because of a lack of evidence that it improves outcomes and because of potential harms from false-positive findings.[24][25][26]

Two large randomized trials involving over 388,000 women

• Found that breast self-examination does not reduce breast cancer deaths[27]

• It almost doubles the number of benign (non-cancerous) biopsies performed, causing unnecessary anxiety, discomfort, and medical costs[27]

What is recommended instead: Rather than formal breast self-examination, medical organizations recommend "breast self-awareness"—being familiar with how your breasts normally look and feel and promptly reporting any changes to your healthcare provider.[1][24] This is different from a structured monthly self-exam. Approximately 43% to 71% of breast cancers are self-detected by women who notice a change, which supports the value of being aware of your breasts without performing a formal examination.[24]

Bottom line: You do not need to perform a formal monthly breast self-examination. Instead, be aware of how your breasts normally look and feel, and tell your doctor if you notice any changes.

Clinical Breast Examination (CBE)

Why evidence is limited

The USPSTF and AAFP state that there is insufficient evidence to assess the benefits and harms of clinical breast examinations for screening.[25][28] The American Cancer Society (ACS) recommends against routine clinical breast examinations for average-risk women because of insufficient evidence of benefit and a high rate of false-positive results (55 false-positives for every breast cancer detected).[26]

No randomized trials have compared clinical breast examination alone versus no screening to determine if it reduces breast cancer deaths.[28]

What is recommended: While clinical breast examination is not recommended as a standalone screening method, some organizations (including NCCN and ACOG) still recommend it as part of a clinical encounter, particularly for ongoing risk assessment and counseling.[1] If you have a breast concern or symptom, a clinical breast examination is an important part of your evaluation.

Bottom line: Clinical breast examination alone has not been proven to save lives when used for screening. However, it may still be performed as part of your regular healthcare visits for risk assessment and to evaluate any symptoms you may have.

Electrical Impedance Scanning (EIS)

What it is: Electrical impedance scanning measures the electrical properties of breast tissue. The theory is that cancer cells have different electrical conductivity than normal cells.

Why it is not recommended

Systematic reviews have found insufficient evidence to recommend electrical impedance scanning for breast cancer screening.[2] Studies have shown:

• Highly variable sensitivity (26% to 98%) and specificity (8% to 81%)[2]

• Significant heterogeneity among studies, making it difficult to draw reliable conclusions[2]

• High false-positive rates that lead to unnecessary biopsies and anxiety

• No studies demonstrating that it reduces breast cancer deaths[2]

Bottom line: Electrical impedance scanning has not been proven effective for breast cancer screening and is not recommended by medical organizations.

Blood Tests and Biomarkers

Currently, no blood test is approved or recommended for breast cancer screening in average-risk women. While tumor markers such as CA 15-3 and CEA are used in some situations (such as monitoring treatment response in patients with advanced breast cancer), they are not sensitive enough to detect early breast cancer.

Research is ongoing to develop blood-based tests for early cancer detection, including "multi-cancer early detection" (MCED) tests. However, these tests:

• Are still in early stages of development and clinical validation

• Have not been proven to reduce breast cancer deaths

• Are not currently recommended as a replacement for mammography

Bottom line: There is no blood test that can replace mammography for breast cancer screening. If you see advertisements for blood tests claiming to detect breast cancer early, be aware that these are not proven or recommended screening methods.

Other Emerging or Unproven Methods

Several other technologies have been promoted for breast cancer screening but lack sufficient evidence:

• Optical imaging: Uses light to image breast tissue. Not supported by sufficient evidence for screening.

• Elastography: Measures tissue stiffness using ultrasound. While useful as an adjunct to ultrasound for evaluating known breast lesions, it is not recommended for screening.[2]

• Positron emission mammography (PEM): A nuclear medicine technique similar to PET scanning. Not recommended for routine screening due to radiation exposure and limited evidence.

What IS Recommended for Breast Cancer Screening?

The following methods are supported by evidence and recommended by major medical organizations:

For average-risk women

• Mammography (preferably with tomosynthesis/3D mammography) is the foundation of breast cancer screening and is the only method proven to reduce breast cancer deaths.[1]

For women at increased risk

• Mammography plus breast MRI is recommended for women at high risk (≥20% lifetime risk), including those with BRCA mutations or a strong family history.[1]

• Supplemental screening with contrast-enhanced mammography (CEM) or molecular breast imaging (MBI) may be considered for high-risk women who cannot undergo MRI.[1]

• Whole breast ultrasound may be done if contrast-enhanced imaging or functional imaging is not available.[1]
