What Does BI-RADS 3 Mean?

What Does BI-RADS 3 Mean?

Your recent breast imaging showed a finding that has been classified as BI-RADS 3, which means "probably benign." This is a specific category used by radiologists to describe findings that are very likely to be benign (not cancer), but your doctor wants to monitor them with follow-up imaging to confirm they remain stable over time.

The chance that a BI-RADS 3 finding is cancer is 2% or less—meaning that 98 out of 100 of these findings are not cancer.[1][2] This low risk is why your doctor is recommending monitoring rather than an immediate biopsy.

What Types of Findings Are Classified as BI-RADS 3?

Common findings that may be classified as BI-RADS 3 include:[1]

• A smooth, round mass with well-defined borders

• A focal area of asymmetry (an area that looks slightly different from the surrounding tissue)

• A small group of tiny, round calcifications

• A complicated cyst (a fluid-filled sac with some internal features)

What Is the Recommended Follow-Up?

Instead of an immediate biopsy, your doctor will monitor the finding with follow-up imaging. The typical schedule is:[1]

• First follow-up: 6 months after the initial finding

• Second follow-up: 12 months after the initial finding (6 months after the first follow-up)

• Third follow-up: 24 months after the initial finding (1 year after the second follow-up)

If the finding remains stable (unchanged) throughout this monitoring period, it will be reclassified as benign, and you can return to your regular screening schedule.[1]

Why Is Follow-Up Important?

Research shows that most cancers found in BI-RADS 3 lesions are detected at the 6-month follow-up visit.[3][4] This is why attending your 6-month follow-up appointment is especially important. If any changes are seen during follow-up, your doctor may recommend a biopsy at that time.

What If I Prefer a Biopsy Instead?

Some patients prefer to have a biopsy right away rather than wait for follow-up imaging. This is a reasonable option if:[1]

• You are very anxious about waiting

• You have difficulty returning for follow-up appointments

• You and your doctor agree that a biopsy would give you more peace of mind

Discuss this option with your doctor if you have concerns about the follow-up plan.

What Should I Do Between Follow-Up Visits?

• Keep all scheduled follow-up appointments—compliance with the recommended schedule is essential for your safety

• Perform monthly breast self-exams and report any new lumps or changes to your doctor

• Contact your doctor if you notice any changes in your breast before your scheduled follow-up

Remember

A BI-RADS 3 finding is probably benign, with a very low chance of being cancer. The follow-up plan is designed to ensure your safety while avoiding unnecessary biopsies. Most women with BI-RADS 3 findings complete their monitoring period and return to routine screening without ever needing a biopsy.
