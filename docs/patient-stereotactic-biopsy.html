What Is a Stereotactic Breast Biopsy?

What Is a Stereotactic Breast Biopsy?

A stereotactic breast biopsy is a minimally invasive procedure that uses mammography (X-ray imaging) to precisely locate and biopsy an abnormal area in your breast.[1][2] This method is most commonly used to evaluate calcifications (tiny calcium deposits) or other abnormalities that are visible on mammography but cannot be felt or seen on ultrasound.[1][3]

Stereotactic biopsy uses computer technology and X-ray images taken from different angles to calculate the exact three-dimensional position of the abnormality, allowing for very precise needle placement.[1][2]

Why Is Stereotactic Guidance Used?

Stereotactic biopsy is the preferred method when:[1][2][3]

• Calcifications are visible on mammography but not on ultrasound

• A mass or area of concern is seen on mammography but not visible on ultrasound

• The abnormality is too small or too deep to feel

• Precise targeting is needed for accurate diagnosis

This technique has largely replaced surgical biopsy for diagnosing non-palpable breast abnormalities, offering equal accuracy with less invasiveness, lower cost, and better cosmetic outcomes.[2][4]

You will be positioned in one of two ways

• Prone (face-down) table: You lie face-down on a special table with your breast positioned through an opening. This is the most common method and helps prevent fainting.

• Upright unit: You sit in a chair with your breast positioned in the mammography machine.

Procedure Steps

1. Breast compression: Your breast will be compressed between two plates, similar to a mammogram, and will remain compressed throughout the procedure

2. Imaging: X-ray images will be taken from two different angles (usually +15 degrees and -15 degrees from center) to calculate the exact location of the target

3. Numbing: The skin will be cleaned and numbed with local anesthetic—you may feel a brief sting

4. Small incision: A tiny nick (about 5 mm) will be made in the skin

5. Needle insertion: A biopsy needle will be inserted to the calculated depth

6. Sample collection: Multiple tissue samples will be taken (typically 6-12 cores)[3][7][8]

• For calcifications, each sample will be X-rayed to confirm calcium was retrieved[3][6]

• You will hear clicking sounds as samples are collected

7. Marker placement: A small titanium clip will be placed at the biopsy site to mark the location[3][5]

8. Final imaging: X-rays will confirm the marker is in the correct position

9. Compression release: The breast compression will be released, and pressure will be applied to stop bleeding

10. Post-procedure mammogram: You will usually have a mammogram right after the procedure to confirm the marker clip is in the correct location and to document how much of the calcifications were removed[6][9]

11. Bandaging: A sterile bandage will be applied

Procedure time: The entire procedure typically takes 30-60 minutes, though the actual biopsy portion is shorter.[4]

What Types of Needles Are Used?

Core needle biopsy:[10][11][12]

• Uses a 14-gauge automated needle to collect tissue cores

• Typically requires at least 5-6 samples for accurate diagnosis

Vacuum-assisted biopsy (VAB):[11][12][13]

• Uses a larger needle (typically 9-11 gauge) with vacuum suction

• Removes larger tissue samples through a single insertion

• Allows collection of more specimens without repositioning the needle

• Reduces underestimation rates compared to standard core biopsy[11][14]

• Particularly advantageous for calcifications[11][12]

Your doctor will determine which type of needle is most appropriate for your specific situation.

What to Expect After Your Biopsy

Immediately after:[1][15]

• You can usually go home right away

• Most patients (95%) resume normal activities within 24 hours[15]

• Avoid strenuous activity or heavy lifting for 24-48 hours

Common side effects:[1][15][16]

• Bruising is very common and may last up to 5 days or longer (occurs in about 71% of patients)[15]

• Mild tenderness at the biopsy site

• Small hematoma (blood collection) may occur but is usually minor[16]

Pain management

• Over-the-counter pain medication (such as acetaminophen) is usually sufficient

• Apply ice packs (wrapped in a towel) to reduce swelling

• Wear a supportive bra for comfort

When to Call Your Doctor

Contact your doctor if you experience:[5][17]

• Fever (temperature above 100.4°F or 38°C)

• Increasing pain that is not relieved by medication

• Excessive bleeding or a growing lump at the biopsy site

• Signs of infection: increasing redness, warmth, or drainage

How Safe Is Stereotactic Biopsy?

Stereotactic breast biopsy is very safe. In a large study of nearly 5,000 patients:[16]

• 96.2% of complications were mild (mostly minor bruising or bleeding)

• Only 0.3% had clinically significant complications

• Major complications such as severe hemorrhage or infection are extremely rare (less than 1%)[1][5]

The procedure does not cause cosmetic deformity because only small amounts of tissue are removed.[1]

When Will I Get My Results?

Biopsy results are typically available within 3-7 days. Your doctor will contact you to discuss the findings and recommend next steps.[18]

Important note about certain results:[1][3]

• If your biopsy shows atypical ductal hyperplasia (ADH), your doctor will likely recommend surgical excision, as about 23-50% of these cases are upgraded to cancer at surgery[19]

• If your biopsy shows ductal carcinoma in situ (DCIS), surgical excision is recommended, as about 17-28% of cases may have invasive cancer present[19][20]

• If your biopsy result does not match what was expected based on your mammogram (called a "discordant" result), additional biopsy or surgical excision may be recommended[21]

How Accurate Is Stereotactic Biopsy?

Stereotactic breast biopsy is highly accurate:[19][21][22]

• Sensitivity: 91-98% (ability to detect cancer when present)

• Specificity: 98-100% (ability to correctly identify benign lesions)

• Overall accuracy: 96-98%

The accuracy is similar to surgical biopsy but with fewer complications and lower cost.[1][22] Studies show that women diagnosed with breast cancer by core biopsy are more likely to be treated with a single surgical procedure compared to those initially diagnosed by surgical biopsy.[22]

Advantages of Stereotactic Biopsy

Compared to surgical biopsy, stereotactic biopsy offers:[1][2][4][22]

• Less invasive with only a small nick in the skin

• Faster recovery with most patients resuming normal activities within 24 hours

• Lower cost (approximately one-quarter the cost of surgical biopsy)[23]

• No scarring or cosmetic deformity

• Lower complication rates (less than 1% vs. 2-10% for surgery)[22]

• Outpatient procedure performed under local anesthesia

• Accurate diagnosis allowing for better treatment planning

Who may not be a candidate for stereotactic biopsy:[3][5]

• Patients with very small breasts that cannot be adequately positioned

• Lesions very close to the skin or chest wall

• Lesions in extremely posterior locations

• Patients with significant bleeding disorders (though most patients on blood thinners can safely undergo the procedure)

• Patients unable to lie still or cooperate with positioning

In these cases, your doctor may recommend an alternative biopsy method or surgical biopsy.

Blood-thinning medications

Most patients can safely continue their blood-thinning medications during stereotactic biopsy, as significant bleeding complications are very rare. However, always inform your doctor about all medications you take, including aspirin, ibuprofen, and supplements.[24]

Follow-Up After Benign Results

If your biopsy shows benign (non-cancerous) findings that match your mammogram appearance, you will typically:[20][25]

• Return to regular screening mammography

• May have follow-up mammograms at 6, 12, and 24 months to ensure stability

• Continue routine breast cancer screening appropriate for your age and risk factors

Remember

• Stereotactic breast biopsy is a safe, well-established procedure with very high accuracy

• The procedure is performed by specially trained radiologists or surgeons[26]

• Complications are rare, and most patients experience only minor bruising

• The procedure provides accurate diagnosis while avoiding the need for surgery in most cases

• Almost all patients (97%) report they would return for another stereotactic biopsy if needed in the future[15]

• Your healthcare team is available to answer any questions or concerns you may have
