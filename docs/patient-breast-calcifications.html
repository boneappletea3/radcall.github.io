What Are Breast Calcifications?

What Are Breast Calcifications?

Breast calcifications are tiny deposits of calcium that can form in breast tissue. They are very common and are usually found on mammograms, where they appear as small white spots. Most breast calcifications are benign (not cancerous) and are a normal part of aging or result from previous breast conditions.[1][2]

There are two main types of breast calcifications:

• Calcium oxalate - almost always associated with benign (non-cancerous) conditions

• Calcium phosphate (hydroxyapatite) - can be found in both benign and malignant (cancerous) lesions[2][3]

What Causes Breast Calcifications?

Breast calcifications can develop for many reasons, including:[1][4]

• Normal aging of breast tissue

• Previous breast injuries or inflammation

• Fibrocystic breast changes (common benign breast changes)

• Fibroadenomas (benign breast lumps)

• Fat necrosis (breakdown of fatty tissue)

• Ductal carcinoma in situ (DCIS) or invasive breast cancer

When calcifications are found on your mammogram, your radiologist will carefully examine their appearance, including their:[5][6]

• Size and shape - Round, uniform calcifications are usually benign, while irregular or branching shapes may be more concerning

• Number and distribution - Scattered calcifications throughout the breast are typically benign, while clustered or grouped calcifications may need further evaluation

• Pattern - Linear or branching patterns following the breast ducts may be more suspicious

Your radiologist will assign a BI-RADS category to describe the findings:[7]

• Category 1 or 2 (Negative or Benign) - No evidence of cancer; routine screening recommended

• Category 3 (Probably Benign) - Less than 2% chance of cancer; short-term follow-up imaging recommended (usually at 6 months)

• Category 4 (Suspicious) - Biopsy recommended to determine if cancer is present

• Category 5 (Highly Suggestive of Malignancy) - Very high probability of cancer; biopsy needed

What Happens Next?

The next steps depend on how your calcifications appear on the mammogram:[7]

If calcifications appear benign: You will return to routine screening mammograms.

If calcifications are probably benign: You will have follow-up mammograms at shorter intervals (typically 6, 12, and 24 months) to ensure they remain stable.[7]

If calcifications are suspicious: Your doctor will recommend a biopsy. This is usually done with a stereotactic core needle biopsy, which uses mammography guidance to obtain small tissue samples. This procedure can be done in the radiology department and does not require surgery in most cases.[4]

Important Points to Remember

• Most breast calcifications are benign. In screening studies, about 48-52% of biopsied calcifications turn out to be non-cancerous.[8]

• Calcifications are an important early detection tool. About 80% of ductal carcinoma in situ (DCIS), an early form of breast cancer, is detected through calcifications on mammography.[9]

• Finding calcifications does not mean you have cancer. Many benign breast conditions cause calcifications, including fibrocystic changes (32.5%), fibroadenomas (30%), and other benign changes.[4]

• Follow your doctor's recommendations. Whether you need routine screening, follow-up imaging, or a biopsy, following the recommended plan is important for your breast health.

Questions to Ask Your Doctor

• What type of calcifications do I have?

• What is my BI-RADS category?

• Do I need additional imaging or a biopsy?

• When should I schedule my next mammogram?

• Are there any symptoms I should watch for?

Remember, your healthcare team is here to answer your questions and guide you through any additional testing that may be needed.
