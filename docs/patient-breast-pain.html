What Is Breast Pain?

What Is Breast Pain?

Breast pain, also called mastalgia, is one of the most common breast complaints among women. Up to 70% of women experience breast pain at some point in their lives.[1] The good news is that breast pain is usually not a sign of anything serious and often improves on its own.

Types of Breast Pain

Breast pain is generally divided into three categories:[1][2]

Cyclical Breast Pain

• The most common type

• Related to your menstrual cycle

• Usually occurs in the late luteal phase (1-2 weeks before your period) and improves when your period starts[2]

• Typically affects both breasts

• Often described as heaviness, tenderness, or aching

• May be part of premenstrual syndrome (PMS)

• Most common in women in their 20s, 30s, and 40s

Non-Cyclical Breast Pain

• Not related to your menstrual cycle

• May be constant or come and go randomly

• Often affects only one breast or a specific area

• May be caused by cysts, trauma, or inflammation

• More common in women in their 40s and 50s

Extramammary Pain (Pain from Outside the Breast)

• Pain that feels like it's in the breast but actually comes from nearby structures

• Common causes include:[3]

• Chest wall or rib pain

• Muscle strain

• Costochondritis (inflammation where ribs meet the breastbone)

• Nerve pain from the spine or neck

• Shingles (herpes zoster)

• Heart, gallbladder, or stomach problems (referred pain)

Is Breast Pain a Sign of Breast Cancer?

Breast pain is rarely caused by breast cancer.[1][4][5]

This is one of the most important things to understand

• Studies show that breast pain alone is not associated with an increased risk of breast cancer.[5][6][7]

• In one large study of nearly 1,000 women who came in with isolated breast pain, the cancer detection rate was only 0.8%—similar to the rate found in routine screening of women without symptoms.[5]

• Another study found that breast pain was actually more common in women without cancer than in women with cancer.[8]

• The vast majority of women with breast pain do not have breast cancer and will not develop breast cancer.[7][8]

However, breast pain should still be evaluated, especially if it is:[9]

• Persistent (lasting more than 4-6 weeks)

• Focal (located in one specific spot)

• Associated with other symptoms like a lump, skin changes, or nipple discharge

Your doctor may recommend imaging to make sure there is no underlying cause that needs treatment, and to provide you with reassurance.

What Causes Breast Pain?

Many factors can contribute to breast pain:[1][3][10]

Hormonal Factors

• Normal hormonal fluctuations during your menstrual cycle

• Hormone replacement therapy

• Birth control pills or hormonal IUDs

Lifestyle Factors

• Ill-fitting or unsupportive bra[1][3]

• Large breast size or breast sagging[10]

• Caffeine (tea, coffee)[10]

- Smoking[10][11]

• Alcohol use[10]

• Stress and anxiety[1]

• Weight gain or obesity[10]

Medical Causes

• Breast cysts

• Fibrocystic breast changes

• Breast infection (mastitis) or inflammation

• Prior breast surgery or biopsy

• Medications (certain hormones, some antidepressants, some heart medications)

• Thyroid problems[12]

• Iron deficiency[12]

When Should I See a Doctor?

Contact your healthcare provider if you have:[9]

• Breast pain that persists for more than 4-6 weeks

• Pain that is focal (in one specific spot)

• A lump or thickening in your breast

• Skin changes such as redness, dimpling, or puckering

• Nipple discharge (especially if bloody or from one breast)

• Nipple changes such as inversion or scaling

• Pain that significantly interferes with your daily activities

• Any breast symptom that concerns you

Treatment Options

The good news is that most breast pain improves with simple measures and does not require prescription medication.[1][2]

First Steps (For Most Women)

Reassurance: For many women, simply knowing that breast pain is common and not a sign of cancer provides significant relief. Studies show that 85% of women feel better after being reassured that their pain is not due to a serious condition.[2]

Supportive Bra: A well-fitted, supportive bra can make a significant difference, especially for women with larger breasts. Consider being professionally fitted. A sports bra may help during exercise and even at night if needed.[1][13]

- Acetaminophen (Tylenol)[2]

• Ibuprofen or naproxen (Advil, Aleve)[2][14]

• Topical anti-inflammatory gels (such as diclofenac gel) applied directly to the painful area—studies show this can reduce pain significantly[14]

Lifestyle Modifications

• Reduce caffeine: Some women find that reducing coffee, tea, chocolate, and caffeinated sodas helps[1][10]

• Limit alcohol: Reducing alcohol intake may help[10]

• Stop smoking: Smoking has been linked to more severe breast pain[10][11]

• Maintain a healthy weight: Obesity is associated with more breast pain[10]

• Reduce stress: Stress and anxiety can worsen breast pain[1]

• Apply warm or cool compresses: Some women find this soothing

Supplements (May Help Some Women)

• Evening primrose oil: Contains fatty acids that may help reduce breast pain. Typical dose is 1,000-3,000 mg daily. Studies show mixed results, but it is generally safe with few side effects.[2][12][13]

• Vitex agnus-castus (chasteberry): May help with cyclical breast pain, especially if associated with PMS. Typical dose is 20-40 mg daily for 3 months.[15]

Note: Always talk to your doctor before starting any supplement.

Prescription Medications (For Severe Cases)

If your pain is severe and does not respond to the measures above, your doctor may consider prescription medications:[2][14]

• Tamoxifen: A hormone-blocking medication that can be very effective for breast pain. Usually given at a low dose (10 mg daily) for 3-6 months. Side effects may include hot flashes and menstrual irregularities.[2]

• Danazol: A synthetic hormone that can reduce breast pain. Side effects may include weight gain, acne, and voice changes, so it is usually reserved for severe cases.[2][14]

These medications are rarely needed and are only used when simpler treatments have not worked.

What About Hormonal Treatments?

If you are taking hormone replacement therapy (HRT) or hormonal birth control and experiencing breast pain:[2]

• Talk to your doctor about adjusting your dose or switching to a different formulation

• Lower-dose estrogen preparations may cause less breast pain

• Changing the type of progestin may help

Do not stop your medications without talking to your doctor first.

Tips for Managing Breast Pain at Home

Keep a Pain Diary

• Track when your pain occurs in relation to your menstrual cycle

• Note what makes it better or worse

• This information can help your doctor determine the type of breast pain you have and the best treatment

Try These Self-Care Measures

• Wear a supportive bra during the day and consider a soft bra at night

• Apply a warm compress or heating pad

• Try gentle massage

• Practice relaxation techniques

• Get regular exercise (with a supportive sports bra)

• Reduce caffeine, especially in the week before your period

Key Takeaways

• Breast pain is very common and affects most women at some point

• Breast pain is rarely a sign of breast cancer—studies consistently show that isolated breast pain is not associated with increased cancer risk

• Most breast pain improves on its own or with simple measures like a supportive bra and over-the-counter pain relievers

• See your doctor if your pain is persistent (more than 4-6 weeks), focal, or associated with other symptoms like a lump or skin changes

• Reassurance is often the best treatment—knowing that your pain is not due to cancer can provide significant relief

• Lifestyle changes such as reducing caffeine, wearing a supportive bra, and managing stress can help

• Prescription medications are rarely needed but are available for severe cases that don't respond to other treatments

Questions to Ask Your Doctor

Remember

Breast pain is a common and usually benign condition. While it can be uncomfortable and worrying, it is rarely a sign of breast cancer. Most women find relief with simple measures, and the pain often improves over time. Your healthcare team is here to help you find the best approach for managing your symptoms.

If you have any concerns about your breast health, don't hesitate to contact your healthcare provider.
