What Is an MRI-Guided Breast Biopsy?

What Is an MRI-Guided Breast Biopsy?

An MRI-guided breast biopsy is a specialized procedure that uses magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) to guide a needle to a specific area in your breast to remove tissue samples for testing.[1] This procedure is performed when a suspicious finding is visible only on MRI and cannot be seen on mammography or ultrasound.[1][2]

MRI-guided biopsy is an important diagnostic tool that allows your doctor to obtain tissue samples from areas that would otherwise require surgical biopsy.[3]

Why Is MRI Guidance Needed?

MRI-guided biopsy is recommended when:[1][2][4]

• A suspicious area is seen on breast MRI but not visible on mammography or ultrasound

• The finding is in a location that is difficult to access with other imaging methods

• You are at high risk for breast cancer and screening MRI detected an abnormality

• MRI is being used to evaluate the extent of known breast cancer and identifies additional concerning areas

Breast MRI is the most sensitive imaging method for detecting breast abnormalities, which is why some findings are visible only on MRI.[2]

What Makes This Procedure Different?

MRI-guided biopsy is more complex and time-consuming than ultrasound or mammography-guided biopsies:[1]

• Requires specialized MRI equipment and a dedicated breast coil

• Takes longer (typically 45-90 minutes)

• Requires you to lie face-down and remain very still

• Uses intravenous (IV) contrast to help identify the target area

• Is performed at specialized breast imaging centers with experienced staff[1][2]

What Happens During the Procedure?

Before the biopsy:[1][5]

• You will change into a hospital gown and remove all metal objects (jewelry, watches, hairpins)

• An IV line will be placed in your arm to give contrast dye during the procedure

• You will lie face-down on a special table with your breast positioned through an opening

• Your breast will be gently compressed to keep it stable during the procedure

During the biopsy:[1][5]

• Initial MRI images will be taken after contrast injection to locate the target area

• The radiologist will calculate the exact position of the abnormality using computer software

• The skin will be cleaned and numbed with local anesthetic

• A small incision (about 5 mm) will be made in the skin

• A biopsy needle will be inserted to the calculated depth while you remain very still

• Additional MRI images will be taken to confirm the needle is in the correct position

• Multiple tissue samples will be collected (typically 6-12 cores)[3][6]

• A small marker clip will be placed at the biopsy site[1]

• Final MRI images will confirm the marker placement

After sample collection:[1][7]

• The needle will be removed and pressure applied to stop bleeding

• A sterile bandage will be applied

• You will usually have a mammogram right after the procedure to confirm the marker clip is in the correct location[1][7][8]

• You will be able to get up and get dressed

What to Expect After Your Biopsy

Immediately after:[6]

• You can usually go home the same day

• Plan to have someone drive you home, as you may feel tired or stiff from lying still

• Avoid strenuous activity for 24-48 hours

Common side effects:[1][6]

• Bruising and tenderness are normal and may last up to a week

• Stiffness or soreness from lying in one position

• Mild swelling at the biopsy site

Care instructions

• Keep the biopsy site clean and dry for 24 hours

• Apply ice packs (wrapped in a towel) to reduce swelling

• Take over-the-counter pain medication as needed

• Wear a supportive bra for comfort

When to Call Your Doctor

Contact your doctor if you experience:[1]

• Fever (temperature above 100.4°F or 38°C)

• Increasing pain not relieved by medication

• Excessive bleeding or a growing lump at the biopsy site

• Signs of infection (increasing redness, warmth, drainage)

Important Considerations

MRI safety:[1]

• Tell your doctor if you have any metal implants, pacemakers, or other devices

• Inform your doctor if you are pregnant or might be pregnant

• Let your doctor know if you have kidney problems, as this may affect the use of MRI contrast

Contrast dye

• The IV contrast used during MRI is generally very safe

• Allergic reactions are rare

• Tell your doctor about any previous reactions to contrast dye

Claustrophobia

• Some patients feel anxious in the MRI scanner

• Let your doctor know if you have concerns about enclosed spaces

• Medication to help you relax may be available

When Will I Get My Results?

Biopsy results are typically available within 3-7 days. Your doctor will contact you to discuss the findings and recommend next steps.[1] The accuracy of MRI-guided biopsy is very high, with success rates exceeding 98-99% at experienced centers.[3][4][6]

Why Is MRI-Guided Biopsy Important?

MRI-guided biopsy allows your doctor to:[1][3]

• Avoid unnecessary surgery for benign findings

• Obtain a diagnosis before surgery for cancerous findings, allowing for better surgical planning

• Evaluate areas that cannot be reached by other biopsy methods

• Provide accurate tissue diagnosis with minimal invasiveness

Studies show that MRI-guided biopsy has very low complication rates and is a safe, effective alternative to surgical biopsy for MRI-only findings.[4][6]

Questions to Ask Before Your Procedure

Remember

• MRI-guided biopsy is a specialized procedure performed by experienced breast imaging teams

• The procedure is safe and highly accurate

• While it takes longer than other biopsy methods, it provides essential diagnostic information

• Complications are rare, and most patients experience only minor bruising and discomfort

• Your healthcare team is available to answer questions and address concerns throughout the process
